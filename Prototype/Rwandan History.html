Rwandan History

Rwanda's human occupation began shortly after the last ice age, with early inhabitants including the Twa pygmy hunter-gatherers around 10,000 years ago. By 3000 BC, various groups like Central Sudanic farmers, Eastern Sudanic agro-pastoralists, Mashariki Bantu, and Southern Cushitic pastoralists settled the region, eventually leading to Bantu dominance by 500 BC.

The Twa were later partially displaced by Bantu agriculturalists (ancestors of the Hutu), who cleared forests for settlements, followed by the contested arrival of pastoralist Tutsi. By the 11th century, the area organized into kingdoms, unified in the 19th century under Mwami Rwabugiri through conquest and consolidation. Colonial powers Germany and Belgium allied with the Rwandan court. Anti-colonial and anti-Tutsi sentiments led to independence in 1962, establishing a Hutu-dominated government under President Grégoire Kayibanda. Ethnic tensions escalated after Juvénal Habyarimana's 1973 coup.

In 1990, the Tutsi-led Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF) invaded, sparking civil war and heightening divisions. Habyarimana's 1994 assassination triggered the genocide, killing hundreds of thousands of Tutsi and moderate Hutu. The RPF seized control, imprisoning many Hutu and prompting millions to flee as refugees to the Democratic Republic of the Congo. This led to RPF-backed invasions in 1996 (First Congo War) and later the Second Congo War, the deadliest since World War II, involving multiple African nations.
